Introduction

  • The single most important activity of a manager is making decisions
  • Decision is a
    • Choice among two or more alternatives
    • Rarely a choice between right and wrong
  • Decision maker is whoever makes such a choice
  • Decisions can be made instantly or systematically
  • Decision making is the process of identifying problems and opportunities and resolving them
  • Decisions
    • Some of them need less time, some of them need more time
    • Some of them are right and some of them are wrong
  • Unless you implement, you will never know if the decision you are choosing is right or wrong. Only after the results will you realize if the decision was a right one or a wrong one
  • Manager should strive to make prompt and correct decisions under given situations
  • It has nothing to do with age
  • Managers make decisions even though if they don’t have experience
  • Practice, Perception and ability to grasp helps in making decisions and not age
  • Make decisions whenever we confront situations that warrant decision making
  • Indecision → unable to make decision because of capability and skills
  • Deflecting the decision making is not good
  • No information vs all information
  • Making a wrong decision is better than indecision
    • You won’t know it’s outcome until it’s implemented
    • If there’s no decision → no implementation → no outcome → no learning
    • Rather fail and learn from the mistakes
  • In most cases you must take risk to make the decisions
  • Decisions by themselves are not important, they are valuable only when they are implemented and lead to results

Decision Making Conditions

Certainty

  • Information is there but outcomes are not sure

Risk

  • Able to define the problem
  • Alternatives are known but the outcome of these alternatives is not known
  • Objective probability → historical data
  • Subjective probability → intuition and experience of the manager

Uncertainty

  • Information is not available
  • We don’t know if data is accurate
  • Outcomes are unknown
  • Managers use creativity
  • Example: Probability of someone being in a relationship
  • Example: Masters in US, looking at various universities, information about all unis are not correct (uncertainty)
  • Information is there but not reliable

Ambiguity (Wicked Problem)

  • Not clear with the problem
  • All kinds of issues → Problem Level, Alternatives
  • Dynamic Environment
  • Even if I know the problem, am not able to look for a solution
  • Example: There might be a local bund → you don’t know if it’ll happen or not. Like the problem itself might happen or might not happen

Steps for Making Decisions

Pre Steps

  • Existing of a problem or opportunity
  • Have willingness to decide and implement

Step 1: Identify the Problem

  • Problem is a discrepancy between existing and desired connection
  • Problem identification is subjective (One’s Problem is Other’s Opportunity)
  • Effectively identifying, defining, framing the problems is important but not easy
  • Make sure it’s a problem and not just a symptom of a problem
  • Define the problem i.e. limit the problem
  • Determine the objectives

Step 2: Identify Decision Criteria

  • Used for evaluating alternatives
  • Define what is important or relevant to resolving a problem
  • Some criteria may be more important to you than others, therefore assign weightage

Step 3: How the Decision should be Made

  • Make decision by yourself
    • With whatever information is available
    • Collect information but the need is not shared and then make decision
    • Collect information, tell them the need and ask them recommendations but you take the final call
  • Group Decision Making
    • Be part of the group and make the decision
    • Allow the group to take a decision and go by the same
    • Generally in those decisions where there is a huge impact
    • Within group decision making we should put some norms just incase no one goes overboard

Step 4: Collect Facts

  • What facts we need? Who has it? Segregate opinions from facts and make assumptions if needed
  • Filter Opinions → based on your experience
  • Decision making should be based on facts but your decision implementation can be based on opinions as well
  • Our Opinion should not be biased
  • A lot of time, information is always incomplete→ sources also might be there
    • Don’t complain and not make a decision → then get the information
  • Try to make assumptions by extrapolating assumptions and ensure it is not very far from reality
  • Don’t make an assumption which is convenient to you → Also don’t make conditional assumptions

Step 5: State the boundaries

  • Constraints and Limiting Factors
  • Constraints are known → have to operate within these
  • Limiting factor is forcing you at obstacles → all possible obstacles
  • Overcome limiting factors but should be within the constraints

Step 6: Develop Alternatives

  • List all possible options
  • Don’t exclude alternatives based on probability meaning don’t exclude those which might show up later on
  • Do nothing or taking no action is itself an alternative
  • Always include 2 additional alternatives
    • Maintain status quo → continue to do what you are doing now → maintain current position
    • Do nothing → don’t bother with competition

Step 7:

  • Analyze each against the given criteria
  • Write down arguments for and against
  • Assess the potential consequences of choosing each alternative
  • Consider both qualitative and quantitative analysis
  • There could be an objective or a subjective approach

Step 8: Select the Best alternative

  • Choose the best alternatives
  • Review the process to ensure flawlessness
  • Ask what can go wrong
  • Identify a contingent decision → second best alternative
  • Ensure not to pass the buck → don’t pass on the accountability to someone else

Step 9: Implement the Alternative

  • Very few books talk about this
  • Reassess the environment for any changes
  • Develop an implementation plan (Plan for action)
    • List of activities to be performed
    • Sequence of activities is important
    • Also take into consideration → other resource requirements or implementors
  • Develop criteria for evaluation
    • How to test your plan
  • Organize execution of plan
  • Take note of the timing
    • Providing necessary resources
    • Giving motivation to your team
  • Activate the plan
  • Evaluate results
  • Recognize performance
    • People who helped implement it should be given the deserved credits

Step 10: Evaluating the Decision Effectiveness

  • See whether the problem is resolved or not
  • If the problem still exists, then assess what went wrong
    • Was the problem incorrectly defined
    • Were errors made when evaluating alternatives
      • Used wrong data
      • Took a wrong time stamp
      • Wrong resources were used
    • Was the right alternative selected but poorly implemented?
      • Resources were not given

Group Decision Making

When to involve?

  • When you need them to accept and understand it
  • When it affects the group itself
  • When they will be involved in implementing it
  • The group is able and willing to become involved

Degrees of Involvement

  • The teams comes to you with a problem and proposed solution
  • Outline the problem and constraints and hand it over to the team to solve
  • Make the decision with your team
  • Ask for the opinion from the team and then make the decision
  • Make the decision and inform the team

NOTE: Bottom to top, involvement of the group increases

Methods and Techniques

  • Engage in Rigorous Debate
    • Devil’s advocate → Did you check that, did you miss that part
    • Point - Counter Point → Here both of them are exact opposites of each other
  • Dialectical inquiry
    • Multiple groups → each will present one solution
    • All other groups will listen and argue and present their views
    • Small sub groups → more divergent views
  • Brainstorming
    • No criticism
    • Don’t leave until you come up with alternatives
  • Delphi Technique
    • Identify experts
    • Go through multiple rounds and none of the experts know each other
    • In first round, we collate information
    • In second round, we give these info to these experts and they give you arguments

Intuitive Decision Making

  • Making the decision on the basis of experience, feelings and accumulated judgement but without conscious thought
  • Enable managers to quickly identify solutions without going through painstaking computations
  • Based on an ability to recognize patterns at lightning speed
  • Based on yours of practice and hands on experience
  • Like profs know where to find mistakes and where to find solutions for the same

Decision Making Biases and Errors

  • Overconfidence
  • Immediate Gratification
  • Anchoring Effect
    • First information, first idea → you prefer this only and not the actual data
  • Confirmation Bias
    • You would want to support your beliefs rather than evaluating it
  • Framing
    • What you want to see and what you don’t want to see
    • How do you perceive things
  • Availability
    • Recent events influence your decision
  • Representation
    • Analogy, comparison with some situation which is incorrect or you fail to compare with some situation that is correct
  • Randomness
    • Superstition or misinterpretation → inferences
  • Sunk Costs
    • You don’t want to pull out but want to justify what you already spent
  • Self serving
    • Claiming yourself if it’s a success but if it fails you blame others
  • Hindsight
    • You believe you are able to predict an event and based on this you make the decision
