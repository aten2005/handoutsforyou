Loanable Funds Theory

  • A theory of interest rate determination that views equilibrium interest rates in financial markets as a result of the supply of and demand for loanable funds

Supply of Loanable Funds

  • Household sector
  • Governments
  • Foreign Investors

Demand of Loanable Funds

  • Households
  • Business
  • Governments
  • Foreign participants

Equilibrium Interest Rate

  • The interest rate that equates the aggregate quantity of loanable funds supplied with the aggregate quantity of loanable funds demanded is called the equilibrium interest rate

Factors that cause shift in supply and demand curves

Supply of Funds

  • Wealth → As wealth increases, supply of funds increases and interest rate decreases
  • Risk of financial security → As risk increases, supply of funds decreases and interest rate increases
  • Near-term spending needs → When these increase, supply decreases so interest increases
  • Monetary Expansion → When policies restrict economic expansion, it decreases supply of funds so interest rate increases as some

Demand of funds

  • Utility derived from assets purchased with borrowed funds → As utility increases, the need for money increases and demand increases and so interest rate increases

Determinants of interest rates for indivdiual securities

Inflation

  • Inflation is the continual increase in the price level of a basket of goods and services
  • IP → Inflation percentage
  • Higher the level of actual or expedited inflation, higher will be the level of interest rates
  • The intuition behind the positive relationship between interest rates and inflation rates is that an investor who buys a financial asset must earn a higher interest rate when inflation increases to compensate for the increased cost of forgoing consumption
  • Higher the inflation, higher the price and so interest rate is higher

Inflation Percentage = CPI(t+1) - CPI(t)/CPI(t) * 100

Real Risk Free Rates

  • Interest rate that would exist on a risk free security if no inflation were expected over the holding period
  • The higher society’s preference to consume today. the higher the real risk free rate will be

Fisher Effect

  • The relationship between Real interest rate, expected inflation and nominal interest rate (i)
    • i = RIR + E(IP) + (RIR*EIP)
    • i = RIR + EIP
    • RIR = i - EIP

Default or Credit Risk

  • Risk that security issuer will default on making it’s promised interest and principal payments to the buyer of the security
  • Higher the risk, higher the interest rate
  • DRP_j = i_jt - i_tt where ijt = interest rate on a security issued by a non treasury and tt is a security issued by the treasury

Liquidity Risk

  • A highly liquid asset is one that can be sold at a predictable price with low transaction costs and thus can be converted into it’s full market value at short notice
  • If a security is illiquid, investors add liquidity risk to it, namely Liquidity Risk Premium

Special Provisions or Covenants

  • For example, for investors, interest payments on municipal securities are free of federal, state, and local taxes. Thus, the interest rate demanded by a municipal bond hold is smaller than that on a comparable taxable bond—for example, a Treasury bond, which is taxable at the federal level but not at the state or local (city) levels, or a corporate bond, whose interest payments are taxable at the state and local levels as well as federal levels.

Term to Maturity

  • This relationship is often called the term structure of interest rates or the yield curve
  • The change in required interest rates as the maturity of a security changes is called the maturity premium

Term Structure

Unbiased Expectations Theory

  • In equilibrium, the return to holding a four-year bond to maturity should equal the expected return to investing in four successive one-year bonds.
  • If this equality does not hold, an arbitrage opportunity exists.
  • For example, if the investor could earn more on the one-year bond investments, he could short (or sell) the four-year bond, use the proceeds to buy the four successive one-year bonds, and earn a guaranteed profit over the four-year investment horizon
  • Thus, according to the unbiased expectations hypothesis, if future one-year rates are expected to rise each successive year into the future, then the yield curve will slope upwards

Liquidity Premium Theory

  • Based on the idea that as investors will hold on to securities as long as they are being compensated for the risk they possess

Market Segmentation

  • Individual investors and FIs have specific maturity preferences, and to get them to hold securities with maturities other than their most preferred requires a higher interest rate (maturity premium)
