Gross Domestic Product

  • Total market value of a country’s output
  • It is the market value of all final goods and services produced within a given period of time by factors of production located within a country

Final Goods and Services

  • Intermediate goods are produced by one firm for use in processiong and resale by another firm
  • Intermediate goods should not be counted in GDP
  • We avoid double counting by counting only the value added to a product by each firm in it’s production process
  • In calculating GDP, we can sum up the VALUE ADDED or we can take the value of final sales

Exclusion of used goods and paper transactions

  • GDP does not count transactions in which money or goods changes hands and in which no new goods and services are produced
  • This is because these are juse exchange of ownership
  • Even if you sell and get profit this doesn’t count in current production

Exclusion of Output produced abroad by Domestically owned Factors of Production

  • GDP is the value of output produced by factors of production located within a country
  • Measure of output produced by factors of production owned by a country’s citizens regardless of where it’s produced is called GDP

Calculating GDP

  • One way is to add up total amount spent on all final goods and services
  • This is called expenditure approach
  • Other way is to add up income received by all factors of production and this approach is called the income approach

Expenditure Approach

  • Personal Consumption Expenditure - C
  • Gross private domestic investment - I - spending on new capital equipment etc.
  • Government consumption and gross investment - G
  • Net Exports = EX - IM where IM means imports
  • GDP = C + I + G + (EX - IM)

Personal Consumption Expenditures

  • Durable Goods
  • Non Durable Goods
  • Services

Gross Private Domestic Investment

  • Refers to purchases of stocks, bonds or mutual funds
  • Total investment in capital by the private sector is called gross private domestic investment
  • Expenditures by firms for machines, tools and so on is the non residential investment
  • Expenditures for houses, apartments is the residential investment
  • Third component is the change in business inventories
  • GDP = Final Sales + Change in Business Inventories
  • Gross investment is the total value of all newly produced capital goods
  • Net investment is equal to the gross investment minus the depreciation
  • Capital(end) = Capital(Begin) + Net Investment

Reason for including Exports

  • If a US firm produces computers and sells them in germany, the computers are part of US production and should be counted as part of US GDP

Income Approach

  • National Income - Sum of eight income items
    • Compensation of employees - wages, salaries
    • Proprietor’s inccome
    • Rental income
    • Corporate profits
    • Net interest - interest paid by business
    • Indirect Taxes minus subsidies - includes sales taxes, custom duties and license fees
    • Net business transfer payments
    • Surplus of government enterprises
  • National income is the total income of the country and not quite GDP
  • GNP less depreciation is called Net National Product
  • GDP - Depreciation = National Income

Nomincal vs Real GDP

  • When we measure something in current dollars, we refer to it as the nominal value
  • Nominal GDP is measured in current dollars
  • Current dollars is the current price we pay
  • To calculate read GDP, we use something called the weight
  • We pick a base year and use the prices in that year to calculate real GDP
  • This is a fixed weight procedure

Limitations of GDP Concept

GDP and Social Welfare

  • GDP does not consider most non market and domestic activities
  • Some increases in social welfare lead to decrease in GDP

Informal Economy

  • Most illegal transactions are missed unless they are laundered into legitimate business
  • Income that is earned but not reported for tax purposes is missed
  • The part of the economy that should be counted in GDP but is not is sometimes called the informal economy
  • GDP does not count this and so is misleading

Gross National Income per Capita

  • GNI is the GNP converted into dollars using an average of currency exchange rates over several years adjusted for rates of inflation
