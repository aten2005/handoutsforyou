Introduction

  • Economics is divided into 2 parts, micro and macro where in micro deals with household and firms but macro deals with the aggregate POV.
  • Economy is the study of how societies, governments, businesses, households and individuals allocate their scarce resources for maximum utility.
    • Government - Limited Fund: Subsidies to be continued?
    • Students - limited Time: Which classes to attend or whether to attend them at all
  • Study of economics can also help in deciding which courses, which career to take/follow

Freebie Debate

  • Limited Resources with government: spending for one purpose reduces funds for the other purpose
  • Distribution of saree(s) and untargeted subsidies are not an addition to the productivity of the economy
  • It is necessary to distinguish freebies from public/merit goods.
  • Expenditure on these brings economic benefits, such as PDS, EGS state’s support for education and health
  • Provision of free electricity, free water, free public transportation are often regarded as freebies, which potentially undermine credit culture, distort prices through cross subsidization eroding incentives for private investment.
  • Some welfare schemes we have right now were previously freebies
  • Some welfare schemes with serious negative consequences- environmental costs of groundwater exploitation due to free electricity, overconsumptions/wastage of free goods/services etc.
  • The objective of most democratic governments is to get re-elected
  • Free goods - instant handouts to public while infrastructure is long term investments
  • Money/goods/services directly to public vs welfare improvement indirectly through more jobs, better infrastructure
  • We finance your freebies through high taxes or public debt
  • Fiscal Imprudence - High public debt can be dangerous for economy (case of Sri Lanka - low taxes but high govt expenditure)
  • Over subsidization of one sector on cost of other sectors - Results in reduction in per capita GDP, high unemployment and deindustrialization

Role of Economics

  • Helps us understand the global and local developments
  • Interconnectedness of various markets can be understood more clearly with economics concepts.
  • Demand Curve tells us how price and quantity relate to each other for demand, similarly Supply Curve - Demand is a straight line from x axis to y axis and supply is y axis to other quadrant
  • The supply curve is upwards sloping because over time suppliers get to choose the price
  • When we put demand and supply curve together, their intersection point will be the equilibrium price/quantity
  • When demand increases and the demand line moves right. If in this situation there’s a price cap (P ceiling) then the supply automatically decreases
  • In economics we have 2 decision making entities - Household and Firms
  • For a firm to choose between Labor intensive work or capital intensive work it depends on what they need as the output and other factors
  • Household must decide how to divide their incomes among all the goods and services available in the marketplace while the firm must decide what to produce how much to produce and where to locate
  • In a market, firms sell the goods and HH buys the goods while from a capital POV, HH saves money in bank and this is given as loan to firms

How Economics Helps in developing a way of thinking

  • Opportunity Cost
    • The full “cost” of making a specific choice includes what we give up by not making the alternative choice
    • The best alternative that we forgo or give up when we make a choice or decision is called the opportunity cost
    • Accountant’s Cost + Opportunity Cost ⇒ Economic Cost
    • Say a guy leaves his job for his father and his father refuses to pay his salary, so here opportunity cost is the job while the accountant’s cost will include the salary to everyone else except the guy. So the Economic Cost will be the sum of both
    • Arise because resources are limited, say, time
  • Marginalism:
    • Analyzing the additional or incremental costs or benefits arising from a choice or a decision
    • A firm will compare Marginal Revenue (If higher, then profit) with Marginal Cost where marginal is basically implying that we are taking a situation where we actually produce the good
    • But for Household, we check marginal utility and marginal cost (generally price bore by the user)
    • People apply the marginalism concept regularly, even if subconsciously in their private decisions
    • Sensible choice requires comparing marginal utilities and marginal costs
  • Sunk Costs
    • These cannot be recovered if a business decides to leave an industry
    • Money spent on ads, marketing and research and development projects which cannot be carried forward into another market or industry
    • Examples include Airlines, Defense etc.
    • Not part of firm’s decision
  • Efficient Markets
    • A market in which profit opportunities are eliminated almost instantaneously
    • Let’s say we have a stock, demand is increased and so price is increased. If it’s efficient, then the opportunity of making profit is eliminated
    • Also known as No Free Lunch

Method of Economics

  • These are of two types: Positive and Normative
  • Positive attempts to understand behavior and the operation of economic systems without making judgements as to whether the outcome will be good or bad
  • Describes what exists and how it works
  • Normative Economics will look into the outcome of the economic behavior and asks whether they are good or bad and whether they can be made better
  • This involves judgements and prescriptions for courses of actions
  • Also known as policy economics

Theories and Models

  • A model is a formal statement of a theory, and is usually a mathematical statement of a presumed relationship between two variables
  • A variable is just any measure that changes from time to time
  • Ockham’s razor → The principle that irrelevant detail should be cut away

Economic Policy

  • Depends on 4 factors: Efficiency, Equity, Growth and Stability
  • In economics, efficiency means allocative efficiency. An efficient economy is one that produces what people want at the least possible cost. If the system allocates resources to the production of goods and services that nobody wants, it is inefficient.
  • If all members of a particular society were vegetarians and somehow half of all that society’s resources were used to produce meat, the result would be inefficient
  • Equity defines fairness from the POV of the beholder
  • Economic Growth is the measure of increase in the total output of the economy
  • If output grows faster than population, output per person increases and standards of living increase

Cautions and Pitfalls

  • Interested in cause and effect but sorting out causality from correlation is not always easy

Post Hoc Fallacy

  • “after this (in time), therefore because of this”
  • If event A happened before B, it is not necessarily true that A caused B
  • Erroneous link between correlation and causation
  • Two variables are said to be correlated if one variable changes when the other changes
  • However correlation doesn’t imply causation

Fallacy of Composition

  • The erroneous belief that what is true for a part is necessarily true for the whole
