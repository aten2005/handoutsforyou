Assumptions

We start of this chapter with:

  • Perfect Knowledge: The assumption that households possess a knowledge of the qualities and prices of everything available in the market and that firms have all available information concerning wage rates, capital costs, technology, and output prices.
  • Perfect Competition: An industry structure in which there are many firms, each being small relative to the industry and producing virtually identical products, and in which no firm is large enough to have any control over prices.

Diamond Water Paradox

  • A paradox stating that (1) the things with the greatest value in use frequently have little or no value in exchange and (2) the things with the greatest value in exchange frequently have little or no value in use
  • Marginal Value of water is 0

Income and Substitution Effects

Income Effect

  • The change in consumption of the original product due to this improvement in well-being, or your feeling richer, is called the income effect of a price change
  • The increase or decrease in purchases of a good when its price changes that come from a change in how rich or poor you feel as a result of that price change is called the income effect

Substitution Effects

  • A fall in the price of product X might cause a household to shift its purchasing pattern away from substitutes toward X. This shift is called the substitution effect of a price change
  • When price of a product falls, even though income decreases, since opportunity cost of buying the product would have decreased, there’s high chance of you buying the product

Labor Supply Decision

  • If you don’t want to work for wage
    • You can not work
    • You can work for free
  • Wage rate can be thought of as the opportunity cost when you prefer not to work and stay at home
  • You can compare with marginal utility of leisure relative to it’s price

Price of Leisure

  • For each hour of leisure you have, you sacrifice wage, so wage rate is price of leisure
  • If income of labor increases, they earn more and they have money to spend on leisure and if leisure is a normal good, an increase in income causes increases in leisure and then we get lower labor supply → Income effect of wage increase
  • There is also Substitution effect of wage increase → If leisure is too expensive, then people will substitute other goods for leisure and this would mean more working and less demand for leisure and higher labor supply

Saving and borrowing

  • When a person saves or borrows, he is dependent on his future expectations.
  • Some people save money for the future when they might not work and have plans to retire
  • Saving is for future consumption
  • Borrowing is for current consumption
  • Let us check how substitution and income effect Saving
  • Let us start with substitution effect, Price of current consumption = interest rate and if interest rate increases so we see that the price of spending every dollar right now will increase due to opportunity cost of saving that dollar in a bank
  • So we end up substituting money spent now with money put in bank
  • Now similarly, let’s go into income effect. Say interest rate increases, since less time is now needed to get the same amount of money, saving decreases
  • The amount of capital available in the market is constrained by how much people are saving

That is, as more of X and less of Y are consumed, MUx/MUy declines. As you consume more of X and less of Y, X becomes less valuable in terms of units of Y, or Y becomes more valuable in terms of X. This is almost but not precisely equivalent to assuming diminishing marginal utility
