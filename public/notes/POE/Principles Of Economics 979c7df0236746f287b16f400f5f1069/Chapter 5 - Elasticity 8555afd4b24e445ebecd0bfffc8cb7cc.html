Introduction to Elasticity

Resources: Untitled 1 (mnstate.edu)

  • Elasticity quantifies as how sensitive one variable is to change in the second
  • We often consider responsiveness or elasticity by looking at prices
  • Price elasticity of demand is how demand responds to price changes
  • Same with supply but if price of good A changes demand of B, this is called cross price elasticity of demand
  • If % change in quantity > % change in price, then total revenue increases
  • If % change in quantity < % change in price, then total revenue decreases

Price Elasticity of Demand

  • When prices rise, the quantity demanded can be expected to decline
  • When prices fall, quantity demanded can be expected to rise
  • Price elasticity of demand is simply the ratio of percentage change in quantity demanded to the percentage change in price
  • price elasticity of demand = % change in quantity demanded / % change in price

Types of Elasticity

  • Elasticity of demand can vary from 0 to negative infinity
  • An elasticity of 0 indicates that quantity demanded doesn’t change wrt price change
  • A demand curve in which even the smallest price increase reduces quantity demanded to zero is known as a perfectly elastic demand curve
  • A special case is when elasticity is -1, where the demand change is exactly same as price change
  • NOTE: It’s generally that demand elasticities are negative
  • Perfectly elastic is where quantity drops to zero even at slight increase in price
  • Perfectly inelastic is where even if price increases a lot, demand doesn't decrease a lot

Midpoint Formula

  • In many cases we tend to use the average of variables instead of using initial values
  • This is called midpoint formula
  • Elasticity changes from point to point even though it has constant slope
  • As we move down a typical straight line demand curve, price elasticity falls. Demand becomes less elastic as price is reduced
  • When they say arc elasticity use this only

Effect of change of price

  • For an elastic good, there are alternatives i.e. there are substitutes, so if price increases, the quantity demanded decreases a lot and so the total revenue reduces
  • But for an in-elastic good, there are no alternates, so if price increases also quantity demanded won’t fall too much, so total revenue still increases
  • The quantity of oil demanded is not as responsive to a change in price as is the quantity of bananas demanded. In other words, the demand for oil is more inelastic than is the demand for bananas
  • So for an elastic good, to increase revenue he has to reduce price and for an inelastic good, to increase revenue he has to increase the price

Determinants of Demand Elasticity

Availability of Substitutes:

  1. If there are substitutes and the price increases, the quantity demanded will fall too much, which is basically an elastic good
  1. Opposite is true for an inelastic good → Substitutes are not available

Important of being unimportant

  1. Sometimes there are products which represent a very small budget of our life and when you check price changes at that level, they won’t be significant and so we are not likely to respond to changes like these

Time Dimension

  1. In the longer run, since people can’t afford to keep paying high prices, they shift towards other substitutes which makes it more elastic

Luxuries vs Necessities

  1. Luxuries are elastic while necessities are inelastic

Income Elasticity of Demand

  • Income elasticity can be positive or negative, positive → health and education
  • For negative → birth rate in low income countries
  • +ve is normal and -ve is inferior

Cross Price Elasticity of Demand

  • A positive cross-price elasticity indicates that an increase in the price of X causes the demand for Y to rise → X and Y are substitutes
  • If cross-price elasticity turns out to be negative, an increase in the price of X causes a decrease in the demand for Y → X and Y are complements
  • We check that the prices of everything remain constant excluding good A, and good B quantity changes, good B price should be constant
  • 0 ⇒ Unrelated

Elasticity of Supply

  • The elasticity of supply is a measure of how easily producers can adapt to a price increase and bring increased quantities to market.
  • In output markets, the elasticity of supply is likely to be a positive number—that is, a higher price leads to an increase in the quantity supplied, ceteris paribus
  • But in input markets, we have elasticity of labor supply which is % change in labor supplied by % change in wage rate but the problem arises because: If wages increase a lot, even though it does look like the labor supply will increase, more laborers will become well off and stop working so technically labor supply decreases
  • NOT ALWAYS POSITIVE

How Raise in Taxes: Using Elasticity

Excise Taxes

  • Per unit taxes of sort are called excise taxes
  • When we impose tax (saying for every item a seller sells, they have to pay tax), the supply curve moves left ward(technically upward by the amount of tax) because the tax is the extra cost to the seller which he never had to pay before
  • This leads to fall in demand due to increase in price which is caused by shifting of the curve
  • And so the demand falls, and the new equilibrium price also increases but it might not be sufficient to actually pay the tax from the seller POV
  • Ideally should impose tax on inelastic goods
