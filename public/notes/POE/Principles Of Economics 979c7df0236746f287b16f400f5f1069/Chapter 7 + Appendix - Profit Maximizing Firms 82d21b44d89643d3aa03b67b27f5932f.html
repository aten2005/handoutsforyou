Behavior of Profit Maximizing Firms

  • Objective of household was to maximize utility
  • Objective of firm was to maximize profit and they’ll try to minimize their cost
  • Firm purchases input and use it to produce output
  • Production: Process through which inputs are combined, transformed and turned into outputs
  • Firm: An organization that comes into being when a person or a group of people decide to produce a good or service to meet perceived demand
  • Sometimes, government and household are also counted as Firms
  • Private business firms are set apart from other producers like household and government by their purpose

Assumption

  • Perfect Competition: An industry structure in which there are many firms, each being small relative to the industry and producing virtually identical products, and in which no firm is large enough to have any control over prices.
  • So firms are price taker
  • In perfectly competitive market, new competitors can freely enter and exit the market

Three Decisions of a Firm

  • How much output to supply?
  • How to produce that output?
  • How much of each input to demand?

Choices of firms are interlinked with each other

  • Once a firm has decided how much to produce, the choice of a production method determines the firm’s input requirements
  • Similarly, given a technique of production, any set of input quantities determines the amount of output that can be produced
  • Changing tech, will change relation between input and output quantities
  • A profit maximizing firm chooses the tech that minimizes it’s cost for a given level of output

Profits and Economic Costs

  • Profit is the difference between total revenue and total cost
  • Profit = Total Revenue - Total Cost
  • Total Revenue is basically Price * Quantity Sold
  • But for total cost you should consider: Out of pocket costs and opportunity costs and normal rate of return on capital
  • Economic Profit: Profit that accounts for both explicit costs and and opportunity costs
    • Total Revenue - Total Economic Cost

Out of Pocket Cost

  • Explicit or accounting costs
  • Refer to costs as an accountant would calculate them

Opportunity Cost

  • Implicit Costs
  • We consider this because we are analyzing the behavior of firms from the stand point of a potential investor or a new competitor
  • If I am thinking about a firm or shares in a firm or entering an industry as a new firm, I need to consider full costs of production

Normal Rate of Return

  • Normal rate of return is the rate that is just sufficient to keep owners and investors satisfied between the investment they are doing and their next best alternative investments
  • Opportunity cost of capital
  • It is the annual flow of net income generated by an investment expressed as a percentage of total investment
  • We refer to the rate of return as yield of investment
  • If the rate of return were to fall below normal, it would be difficult or impossible for managers to raise resources and funds
  • Example 1: If we have A and B trying to start a business and they need a pushcart of $2000, each belt is $10 and they estimate to sell 3000 belts. We assume each belt is $5 from supplier and annual wage for pushcart is 14000$
    • Annual Revenue = 30000$
    • Total Cost = 15000$ (3000*5) + 14000$ = 29000$
    • On the basis of annual revenue and cost flows, the firm seems to be making a profit of 1000$
    • But what about the pushcart, this is not a direct part of the cost, the only thing they are giving up is the forgone interest in investment which is 2000$
    • So, we get a loss of 1000$ because 29000→31000
    • We saw that we have 1000$ profit which is 5% and since we have rate of return < rate of normal = 10%, so we’ll have a loss
  • Example 2: Sells 5000 units of output per year at $10, and labor costs per year are 10$, Assume that total labor costs to the firm are 45000$ annually. Assume that the total capital stock is 100000 that the return available to investors with risks is 10%, Is there profit
    • TR = 50000
    • TC = 45000 + Capital Cost 10% = 45000 + 10000 = 55000
    • Therefore loss = 5000
  • Example 3: For 2 years, salary of A and B was 60000$ each and together they had saved 50000$. After 2 years they decided to quit their jobs and start a business. They used 50000$ to buy computer equipment. For next 2 years, they got 40000$ in revenue, paid themselves 10000 annually each and rented office for 18000 per year. Prior to investment their 50000 was in bonds and earning an interest of 10%
    • TR = 40000
    • Explicit Costs = 10000*2 + 18000 = 38000
    • Implicit Cost = (Capital) 5000 + (60000(Opportunity Cost)-10000(new salary))*2 = 105000
    • Therefore loss of 40000 - 142000 = -102000 LOSS :)

Relation between Rate of Return and Normal Rate of Return

  • RoR > NRR → Profit
  • RoR = NRR → 0 Profit
  • RoR < NRR → Loss

Level of Profit and Investment

  • Part of total cost
    • When firms earn a normal rate of return equal to rate of return, zero profit
    • If level is positive, it’s earning more than is sufficient to retain interest of investors
    • And if it has a negative level of profit, the firm will lose investors

Short Run vs Long Run Decisions

Short Run

  • Two assumptions define the short run
    • A fixed scale or a fixed factor of production
    • No entry into or exit from the industry
  • Short run is defined as that period during which existing firms have some fixed factor of production i.e. during which time some factor locks them into their current scale of operations
  • Secondly, new firms cannot enter and existing firms cannot exit an industry in the short run
  • Firm may curtail operations but they are still locked into some costs even though they may be in the process of going out of business
  • Factors of production that are fixed, differ from industry to industry
    • For a manufacturing firm, the size of the physical plant
    • For a private physician, the limit may be that the day has a fixed number of hours
    • For a farmer, the fixed factor may be the land

Long Run

  • There are no fixed factors of production
  • In addition, new firms can start up operations and existing firms can go out of business
  • The point is firms make 2 types of decisions
    • day to day operations
    • longer term strategic plans

Bases of Decisions

  1. Market price of output
    1. Output price determines potential revenues
  1. The techniques of production that are available
    1. This determines the input I need and how much they’ll cost
  1. Prices of input
    1. this determines costs

Optimal Method of Production

  • The production method that minimizes cost

Determining the Optimal Method of Production

  • A firm, with cost determined and market price of output known, a firm will make a final judgement about the quantity of product to produce and the quantity of each input to demand

Production Process

  • Production is the process through which inputs are combined and transformed into outputs
  • Production technology relates inputs to outputs
  • Specific quantities of inputs are needed to produce any given service or good

Production Technology

  • the quantitative relationship between inputs and outputs
  • most outputs can be produced by a number of different techniques
  • labor intensive technology
    • relies heavily on human labor instead of capital
  • capital intensive technology
    • relies heavily on capital instead of human labor
  • Which is more appropriate
    • Firms choose one which minimizes cost of production
    • For a firm in an economy with a plentiful supply of inexpensive labor but not much capital, the optimal method of production will involve labor intensive techniques
    • For a firm in an economy with high wages and high labor costs an incentive to substitute away from labor and to use more capital intensive techniques

Production Function

  • A numerical expression between price and input

Marginal Product and the Law of Diminishing Returns

  • Marginal Product
    • The additional output that can be produced by adding one more unit of a specific input, ceteris paribus
  • Law of diminishing return
    • When additional units of a variable input are added to fixed inputs after a certain point, the marginal product of the variable input decreases
    • Always applied in short run since some factor of production is fixed
    • Started by Ricardo and was applied in agriculture

Marginal Product vs Average Product

  • Average Product of labor = total product/total units of labor
  • Average product follows the marginal product but it does not change so quickly
  • If marginal product is above average product, the average rises and if marginal product is below average product, the average falls
  • The marginal product curve is a graph of the slope of the total product curve of the production function
  • If MP > AP ⇒ AP increases and if MP < AP ⇒ AP decreases and Average Product is max when MP = AP
  • L1 is the capacity constraint
  • Max of AP is the intersection point of MP and AP

Production Functions with two Variable Factors of Production

  • If capital is expensive, they’ll start hiring more and get more labor
  • If labor becomes expensive, capital investment increases
  • So we have complimentary factors
  • In general, additional capital increases the productivity of labor. Because capital is of no use without people to operate it, we say that capital and labor are complimentary inputs

Which Technology to use?

  • The right choice among inputs depends on how productive the inputs are

Determinants of Cost of Production

  • Technologies that are available and
  • Input prices
  • A firm will choose the tech that minimizes the cost of production given current market input prices
