Introduction

  • Things that are produced and then used in production of other goods and services are called capital resources or capital
  • The basic resources available to a society are often referred to as factors of production or simple factors
  • Three key factors are land, labor and capital

Scarcity, Choice and Opportunity Cost

  • Important Questions are WHAT, HOW and WHO

One Person Economy

  • Individual and society are one.
  • The question of who to distribute doesn’t exist here
  • Opportunity Cost: The concepts of constrained choice and scarcity are central to the discipline of economics. They can be applied when discussing
  • If there’s a single guy on an island, he must decide WHAT he wants to produce not what he NEEDS because we don’t define what someone needs
  • He always will face trade offs from the other alternative that’s available and this is called opportunity cost
  • If he becomes comfortable, he may work on making a building where he can stay but this reduces the time available for him to gather food

Two or More Person Economy

  • Works on the concept of specialization, exchange and comparative advantage
  • These 3 will benefit all parties involved even when some are absolutely more efficient than others
  • Both of them can have different preferences and skill sets
  • Preferences about what things to produce are likely to be different
  • Say, Colleen is very good at tracking animals and Bill has a knack for building things
  • Once things are produced, they also have to decide how to divide them
    • One of the other may take the charge
    • The two may agree to cooperate
    • They may go off to live alone at opposite ends of the island and later share the resources by sharing and trading
  • Comparative Advantage
    • The specialization and free trade will benefit all trading parties, even those that may be absolutely more efficient producers
    • A producer has a comparative advantage over another in the production of a good or service if he or she can produce that product at a lower opportunity cost
  • Absolute Advantage
    • A producer has absolute advantage in the production of a good or service if he/she can produce that product using fewer resources (a lower absolute cost per unit)
    • In a situation where one guy, say A is >> the other guy B in everything, then A is said to have absolute advantage over B if A can produce the same thing as B using fewer resources
  • Let’s say we have 2 jobs, cutting logs and gathering food
  • B is able to produce more food while C is able to produce more logs

Weighing Present & Expected Future Costs & Benefits

  • We trade off present and future benefits in small ways all the time
  • Simple Example is the act of saving
  • Consumer Goods → Present Consumption
  • Capital Goods → Goods that already are produced and these will be used to produce other valuable goods and services over time
  • Because resources are scarce, the opportunity cost of every investment in capital is forgone present consumption
  • The process of using resources to produce new capital is called investment

Production Possibility Frontier

  • A graph that shows all the combinations of goods and services that can be produced if all the society’s resources are used efficiently
  • Points that are actually on the curve are points of both full resource employment and production efficiency but not allocation efficiency
  • Production efficiency is a state in which a given mix of outputs is produced at least cost
  • Points in the gray area represent either unemployment or production inefficiency
    • Unemployment:
      • During economic downturns or recessions, industrial plants run at less than their total capacity
      • Moving onto the frontier from a point such as D means achieving full employment of resources
    • Inefficiency
      • Can result from waste and mismanagement from a firm’s operating
      • Policy Paralysis
  • An economy must produce what people want, so in addition to the point being on the curve it has to be on the correct position depending on which axis it’s close to
  • The fact that scarcity exists is illustrated by the negative slope of the PPF → The ratio of change in capital goods to the change in consumer goods is NEGATIVE
  • Law of increasing opportunity cost:
    • Here the point is that say we are going from E to F in the below graph, we have 250 change in capital goods for every 200 change in consumer goods
    • But say the point moves towards producing more capital goods, then the opportunity cost increases drastically since for higher change in consumer goods we’ll get less change in capital goods
    • This is because of the fact that we were already producing max capital goods, and the more we increase production of capital goods we would have to stretch the resources a lot for the same
  • Above the curve situations are not possible
  • Below the curve, we get that it’s inefficient
  • Technology and More Resources lead to a better PPF curve
  • A → All resources given to capital goods
  • B → All resources given to consumer goods

Sources of Growth and Dilemma of Poor Countries

  • Majorly due to Accumulation of Capital and Technological advances
  • For poor countries, capital is essential
  • The resources used to produce capital goods and technological advances can be used to produce consumer goods
  • The more a poor country wants to invest in R&D the less capital it’ll have for consumer goods
  • And this leads to a growing gap between some poor and rich nations
  • On the other hand, rich nations devote a lot of money to capital while a poor country invests in consumer goods

Economic Systems and the Role of Government

Command Economics

  • In this system of economics, the central government answers the economic questions, and sets output targets, incomes and prices
  • Government involvement in theory, can improve efficiency and fairness of the allocation of a nation’s resources
  • A poorly functioning government can destroy incentives, lead to corruption and result in the waste of a society’s resources

Free Economics

  • This is known as laissez-faire economy → which basically means allow them to do
  • The central institution through which a free economy answers the basic questions is the market
  • No role of the government
  • Market is the institution through which buyers and sellers interact and engage in exchange
  • The behavior of buyers and sellers in a laissez faire economy determines what gets produced, how it is produced and who gets it.

Consumer Sovereignty

  • In a free, regulated market, goods and services are produced and sold only if the supplier can make a profit
  • You cannot make a profit unless someone wants to buy the product that you are selling
  • This logic leads to the notion of consumer sovereignty
  • The Idea that consumers ultimately dictate what will be produced(or not produced) by choosing what to produce

Individual Production Decisions - Free enterprise

  • Under a free market system, individual producers must also determine how to organize and coordinate the actual production of their products or services.
  • Often the market system is a free enterprise system
  • It means the freedom of individuals to start private businesses in search of profits
  • Because new businesses require capital before starting up, it’s very risky
  • Proponents of free market systems argue that free enterprise leads to more efficient production and better response to diverse and changing consumer preferences
  • If a producer is inefficient, competitors will come along, fight for the business and eventually take it away
  • Competition forces producers to use efficient techniques of production
  • One of the major themes in economics is the tension between the advantages of free, unregulated markets and the desire for govt involvement
  • Advocates of free markets argue that such markets work best when left to themselves
    • They produce only what they want, without buyers, sellers go out of business
    • Competition forces firms to adopt efficient production techniques
    • Wage differentials lead to people to acquire needed skills
    • Competition leads to innovation in both production techniques and products
  • Disadvantages
    • They do not always produce what people want at the lowest cost: They will form monopolies, which will then control market pricing
    • Rewards(income) may be unfairly distributed
    • Periods of unemployment and inflation recur with some regularity

Distribution of Output

  • In a free market system, the distribution of output - who gets what is also determined in a decentralized way
  • The amount a household gets depends on it’s income and wealth
  • Income is the amount a household earns
  • Wealth is the amount that households have accumulated out of past income through saving or inheritance

Price Theory

  • Price is the amount that a product sells for per unit and it reflects what society is willing to pay
  • Prices of inputs → labor, land and capital → determine how much it costs to produce a product
  • Individuals pursuing their own self interest will go into business and produce the products and services that people want
  • The Basic Coordinating mechanism is price
  • Microeconomic theory is often simply called price theory
  • Many of the independent decisions made in a market economy involve the weighing of prices and costs

Mixed Systems

  • All real systems are mixed i.e. individual enterprise exists and independent choice is exercised even in economics in which the government plays a major role
  • Individual enterprise exists and independent choice is exercised even in economies in which government plays a major role
  • Market economies can’t exist without government intervention namely for production, employment and for redistribution of income
  • But this can also fail:
    • There is no guarantee, a government will actually complete their tasks or they’ll act in own self interest
    • May fail to produce an allocation of resources
