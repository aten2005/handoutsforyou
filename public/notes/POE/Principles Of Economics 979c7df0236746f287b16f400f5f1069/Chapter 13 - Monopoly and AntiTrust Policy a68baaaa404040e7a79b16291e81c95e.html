Imperfect Competition and Market Power

  • In imperfect competition, absence of numerous competitiors and presence of product differentiation creates situations in which firms can at times raise their prices and not lose all their customers
  • Firms are no longer price takers but they are price makers
  • These firms are said to have market power

Firms of Imperfect Competition and Market Boundaries

  • Monopoly is an industry with a single firm in which the entry of new firms is blocked
  • Oligopoly is an industry in which there is a small number of firms each large enough so that it’s presence affects prices
  • Firms that differentiate their products in industries with many producers and free entry are called monopolistic competitors
  • Pure monopoly
    • A single firm that produces a product that has no close substitutes
    • Significant barriers to entry prevent other firms from entering the industry to compete for profits

Price and Output Decisions in Pure Monopoly Markets

  • Price Discrimination means selling the same product to different consumers or groups of consumers at different prices
  • But in the input market, it’s still a price taker

Demand in Monopoly Markets

  • Demand Curve facing such a perfectly competitive firm is thus a horizontal line i.e. it’s perfectly elastic
  • In monopolist, it’s different. It does not constitute a small part of the market. Rather it is the market
  • The firm no longer looks at market price to see what it can change, it sets the market price
  • Monopolist sets it’s price by looking at the trade off in terms of profit earned between getting more money for each unit sold vs selling fewer units

Marginal Revenue and Market Demand

  • We learnt that firms produce as long as marginal revenue exceeds marginal cost
  • A monopolist will also try to maximize profits but the only difference comes in the MR Curve
  • For a competitive firm, MR is simply the price but in case of monopolist, if the firm decides to double it’s output then the market output will double and it is easy to see that the only way the firm will be able to sell is to lower the price
  • For a monopolist, an increase in output involves not just producing more and selling it but also reducing the selling price of the same
  • Therefore MR < P - No Price Discrimination
  • But the reason is
    • Because monopolist is the market, it has control over the price
    • To increase quantity sold, a monopolist must lower its price on each unit sold
    • This has two effects
      • Monopolist makes money on the additional quantity sold
      • The monopolist loses money on the goods that it was already selling as they are now sold at a lower price

Monopolist’s Profit Maximizing Price and Output

  • Max profit = MR = MC
  • We observe that the marginal revenue curve bisects the quantity axis between the total demand curve and origin
  • Because say P = a+bQ = Demand Curve and TR = P*Q = aQ + bQ^2
    • We get MR = a+2bQ

The Absence of Supply Curve

  • In perfect competition, the supply curve of the firm in the short run is same as the portion of firms marginal cost curve that lies above the average cost curve
  • In other words, the amount of output that a monopolist supplies is not independent of the shape of the demand curve.
  • A monopoly firm has no supply curve that is independent of the demand curve for its product.
  • To see why, consider what a firm’s supply curve means. A supply curve shows the quantity of output the firm is willing to supply at each price.
  • If we ask a monopolist how much output she is willing to supply at a given price, the monopolist will say that her supply behavior depends not only on marginal cost but also on the marginal revenue associated with that price.
  • To know what that marginal revenue would be, the monopolist must know what her demand curve looks like

Monopoly and Perfect Competition

  • In perfect competition, P = MR = MC whereas in monopoly P > MR = MC
  • With monopoly, quantity is lower and price is higher
  • Also there is dead weight loss with a monopoly since some potentially beneficial trades do not take place
  • Monopoly is not efficient due to P > MR and leads to a dead weight loss
  • A monopolistic competitor's demand curve is more elastic than a monopolist's or oligopolist's but less elastic than a perfect competitor's. Like basically a monopolistic competitor will not have any fixed price to look for so it’ll be more elastic but in the case of a perfect competitor he has to be inelastic since his price won’t change much

Monopoly in the Long Run and Barriers to Entry

  • For a monopoly to persist, there must be some factors which prevent entry and these are called barriers to entry
  • Technical barriers to entry
    • Economies of scale
    • Large Sunk costs - fixed costs
    • Technical Superiority
  • Legal barriers
    • Patents
    • Franchises awarded by contract
    • Control of a scarce resource
    • Barriers erected by firms to discourage entry

Economies of Scale

  • We described production technologies in which average costs fall with output increases
  • In situations in which those scale economies are very large relative to the overall market, the cost advantages associated with size can give rise to monopoly power
  • When scale economies are so large relative to the size of the market that costs are minimized with only one firm in the industry we have a natural monopoly
  • It comes from having physical apparatus and also comes from marketing and advertising

Government Regulations

  • Government ensures benefits of scale economies are realized without inefficiencies of monopoly power

Government Rules

Price Discrimination

  • We have assumed that the firm faces a known downwards sloping demand curve and must choose a single price and a single quantity
  • Indeed the reason for this difference between a firm and monopoly is that if a monopoly decides to sell more it should lower price
  • In real world, there are many buyers, so we can change prices and this is called price discrimination

Motivation for Price Discrimination

  • If a firm can identify those who are willing to pay a higher price for a good, it can earn more profit from them
  • A firm that charges the maximum amount that buyers are willing to pay for each unit is practicing perfect price discrimination
  • Here the shaded blue area is the consumer surplus since the price is capped at $4
  • Now in a price discriminating firm, the second case happens and that full gray area becomes a monopoly’s profit
